# ISA 200 — Overall Objectives of the Independent Auditor

## Objective

In conducting an audit of financial statements, the overall objectives of the auditor are:

- To obtain reasonable assurance about whether the financial statements as a whole are free from material misstatement, whether due to fraud or error, thereby enabling the auditor to express an opinion on whether the financial statements are prepared, in all material respects, in accordance with an applicable financial reporting framework; and
- To report on the financial statements, and communicate as required by the ISAs, in accordance with the auditor's findings.

## Scope

ISA 200 deals with the independent auditor's overall responsibilities when conducting an audit of financial statements in accordance with ISAs. It sets out the overall objectives, explains the nature and scope of an audit, and explains the scope, authority and structure of the ISAs. It establishes the general responsibilities applicable in all audits, including the obligation to comply with the ISAs.

## Key Requirements

- **Ethical requirements:** The auditor shall comply with relevant ethical requirements, including those related to independence.
- **Professional skepticism:** The auditor shall plan and perform an audit with professional skepticism, recognizing that circumstances may exist that cause the financial statements to be materially misstated.
- **Professional judgment:** The auditor shall exercise professional judgment in planning and performing an audit.
- **Sufficient appropriate audit evidence:** The auditor shall obtain sufficient appropriate audit evidence to reduce audit risk to an acceptably low level.
- **Compliance with ISAs:** The auditor shall comply with all ISAs relevant to the audit and shall have an understanding of the entire text of each ISA.
- **Achieving objectives:** The auditor shall use the objectives stated in relevant ISAs in planning and performing the audit.
- **Departure from requirements:** In exceptional circumstances, the auditor may depart from a relevant requirement if alternative procedures achieve the aim of that requirement (and must document the reasons).

## Key Definitions

- **Reasonable assurance** — A high, but not absolute, level of assurance.
- **Audit risk** — The risk that the auditor expresses an inappropriate audit opinion when the financial statements are materially misstated. A function of risks of material misstatement and detection risk.
- **Risk of material misstatement** — The risk that the financial statements are materially misstated prior to audit. Consists of inherent risk and control risk.
- **Detection risk** — The risk that audit procedures will not detect a misstatement that exists and that could be material.
- **Professional skepticism** — An attitude that includes a questioning mind, being alert to conditions which may indicate possible misstatement due to error or fraud, and a critical assessment of audit evidence.
- **Professional judgment** — The application of relevant training, knowledge and experience in making informed decisions about appropriate courses of action.
- **Misstatement** — A difference between the reported financial statement item and what is required by the applicable financial reporting framework.
- **Applicable financial reporting framework** — The framework adopted by management for the preparation of the financial statements that is acceptable or required by law or regulation.
